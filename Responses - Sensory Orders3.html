Wioleta Kaminska

artist, linguist, educator:
RUNNING OUT OF TIME

We often say that we are running out of time. Overstimulated and overloaded with images and information we suffer from a disintegration of our abilities to see and hear. The accelerated tempo of our living impacts our engagement with the environment. Human-machine interaction is what we   are used to, feel comfortable with and often choose over more personal connection with our most immediate surroundings. As an artist I am interested   in a visual and auditory exploration of seemingly mundane and uneventful environments. Places where time seems to be passing slowly and, yet, where steady and constant change takes place. The goal is to invite the audience to slow down, reflect and experience a sense of wonder and renewed  appreciation for Nature. Because Nature IS running out of patience and time.

Nadia Lichtig

(DE/CZ/FR) artist, pedagogue, researcher:
MOLECULAR LISTENING

I’m driven by the conviction that we belong to an infinite universe, a space governed by cosmological forces. This connection between the living and the cosmos and its forces is something concretized in our bodily memory, which is not always directly accessible to our consciousness. Cosmic forces govern our sensory conditions, but they are often invisible. For instance, we do not see or feel directly the neutrinos coming from the sun and constantly passing through our bodies. I work to create objects with sensory qualities that lead us to a “counter-intuitive” sensory awareness, in order to recognize   the bonds that unite us. A sharpened sensitivity to the biological and chemical conditions of life, of what we are and what constitutes us, the emergence of a sort of “molecular listening,” might make the idea of frontiers at some point obsolete. This could create a sensory awareness that the mineral,  vegetal and animal world are one and the same entity, constantly transforming — all made from stardust.

 

Perfume of Forgetting (2017—ongoing) hand blown glass spheres, hermetically sealed containing ziziphus zizyphus plant extract, print on fabric, case

Yutaka Makino

(JP/DE) artist, researcher:
FORMATION

According to mathematician René Thom’s catastrophe theory, a nonlinear system, such as our perceptual system, exhibits various bifurcations and abrupt shifts with high magnitude by a slight change of parameters. By providing a subtle visual stimulus, the work Formation probes the nonlinearity of our perceptual processes and the multi-stable characteristics of phenomena caused by such nonlinearity. On a monitor screen, a low contrast pattern with minute movements is presented almost below the sensitivity threshold of our central vision. Yet, the faint pattern can be detected by peripheral vision   with its higher contrast sensitivity. When spectators notice a pattern at the edge of their field of vision and try to focus on it, the pattern will dissolve into the white background. Formation puts the spectators in a situation where their techniques of observation need to be reconfigured in order to adapt to a new environment.

Saadia Mirza

(JP/DE) artist, researcher, architect:
SENSING DATA

Working in collaboration with scientists and researchers, I use new media to un-blackbox data that tells stories about war, environment, and climate. I pay attention to sensory impressions of the data – i.e., properties of sound and image experienced in immersion with a narrative that shows how knowledge is created in a world of incomplete information. This practice uncovers a culture of knowledge and expertise in which sensory affordances are more than instinctual – they enable geopolitical and historically situated perspectives on landscapes and territories. What we see or hear, therefore depends on a technique, especially when mediated by sensing technologies that exceed human capacities. Across its range of realistic, surreal and abstract aesthetics, the sensing of landscape is thus as evocative as it is political.

 

Sensing Landscape (2018) 11:26 is a foray into vulnerable and militarized historical landscapes in Afghanistan using archaeological techniques   of remote sensing.

r e a

(Gamilaraay/Wailwan/Biripi/AU) artist, activist:
RESISTING THE INVASION

Right now, in this historical moment, I am making art in an extreme space of social, political & cultural isolation. But how different is this place of being to my usual existence? I am an Indigenous artist from the Gamilaraay/Wailwan/Biripi people. I have been forced (unwillingly) to live and work in a   colonised space called Australia! A space constructed for me by invaders who came with guns, bibles and supposedly civilized ways – every day my senses are bombarded with fragments & shards, the detritus of words & images and the brute force of the privilege & entitlement of ‘whiteness. Like my   fellow Indigenous artists, I make art as cultural and political resistance to this ever oppressive ‘whiteness.’ Our art is an intensely felt but silently staunch connection to and with our traditional lands –we walk, run, stumble, leap and move on country as modern day resistance fighter’s continuously activating and training our senses to resist the INVASION with every fibre of our being.

Philippe Rahm

(CH/FR) architect, artist:
THERMAL VALUES

Originally, the first public spaces had essentially climatic vocations. It was the role of the basilica, the thermae, the church. Social and political life was created around this need to go there to refresh oneself. We met there, we discussed, we exchanged ideas. During the second half of the 20th century, with cheap energy, all these public spaces lost their thermal sense, replaced by individualistic and private responses. There was no longer any need to go to church to cool off and air conditioning was installed in every apartment. But today, the individualistic and energy-consuming model of modernity no longer works in the fight against global warming.   The Climatorium is a new type of public space for the 21st century based on thermal values. The Climatorium offers a cool public space, where people can come and take refuge during the heat waves, meet, discuss, love and exchange.

Valerio Sannicandro

(IT/FR) composer, researcher:
ORDERS OR DISORDERS OF THE SENSES?

Sensory orders or disorder of senses?   How does language (art) find a way to give an order into a labyrinth of the most disparate stimuli? Every attempt creates new realms of significance and builds Aesthetics (that is a language as well) in which the technological domain is often   the mediator. New (ephemeral) worlds appear, with quite different sensory hierarchies, and – maybe – no orders.

Wioleta Kaminska is a Polish-born and US-based media artist, designer, linguist and educator. Her work ranges from photography, drawings, motion graphics to film, video and animations. Kaminska’s meditative video landscapes are both a study in perception and stillness and visual reflection on the intersection between nature, technology and culture. In her work she is interested in exploring a momentary nature of time and space, and the conflicting forces that shape land and humans. 

Nadia Lichtig is an artist currently living in the South of France. In her multilayered work, voice is transposed into various media including painting, print, sculpture, photography, performance, soundscape and song—each medium approached not as a field to be mastered, but as a source of possibilities to question our ability to decipher the present. Visual and aural aspects entangle in her performances.
Lichtig studied linguistics at the LMU Munich in Germany and at the Ecole des Beaux-Arts de Paris, France with Jean-Luc Vilmouth, where she graduated with honours in 2001, before assisting Mike Kelley in Los Angeles, USA the same year. She taught at the Shrishti School of Art and Technology, Bangalore, India as a visiting professor in 2006, at the Ecole des Beaux-Arts of Valence in 2007, and is professor of Fine Arts at the Ecole Supérieure des Beaux-arts of Montpellier (MOCO-ESBA), France since 2009. She has collaborated with musicians who are also visual artists, such as Bertrand Georges (Audible), Christian Bouyjou (Popopfalse), Nicolu (La Chatte), Nina Canal (Ut) and Michael Moorley (The dead C). Nadia Lichtig worked and works under several group names and pseudonyms (until 2009: EchoparK, Falseparklocation, Skrietch, Ghosttrap and Nanana).

Yutaka Makino was born in Tochigi, Japan in 1976. He studied Earth science, computer music and visual arts in Japan, the Netherlands and the USA. Since 2010 he has lived and worked in Berlin. On the basis of research into areas such as phenomenology, experimental psychology, psychoacoustics, neuroscience and systems theory, Makino probes the processes of perception in experimental setups. His performances and installations provide acoustically and visually conditioned environments that make processes of perception tangible to the perceivers and provoke reflection on the acts of perception and the perceivers’ behavioral responses to the environment.

Saadia Mirza is an architect, anthropologist and pedagogue. Her interests and research include landscape studies, the history of cartography, science and technology using filmmaking, animation, virtual reality and digital cartography. She works in collaboration with scientists and researchers, using new media to "“unlock” data sets that tell stories about war, conflict and climate change. Her works pay attention to sensory impressions of data, sound, image and texture - experienced in immersion with a narrative that shows how knowledge is created in a world of incomplete information. Her recent works have been supported by Akademie Schloss Solitude in Stuttgart and the Cité Internationale des Arts in Paris.

r e a is an artist, curator, activist, academic, cultural educator and creative thinker engaged in an arts-lead, research-based practice. A descendant from the Gamilaraay/Wailwan people from the Central West New South Wales Australia and Biripi people from the mid-North Coast of NSW, rea’s ongoing artistic practice and research focuses on creating new Indigenous perspectives which disrupt colonial narratives, prioritize Indigenous voices and explore discourses of intersectionality and positionality through the cultural convergence of Aboriginality with the creative arts and technology, history and colonialism, the body and identity and gender and queer politics. r e a recently completed a Doctorate in Visual Anthropology at the University of New South Wales Art & Design school and works as a lecturer in the Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Unit at the University of Queensland.

Philippe Rahm is a Swiss architect, principal in the office of “Philippe Rahm architectes”, based in Paris, France. His work, which extends the field of architecture from the physiological to the meteorological, has received an international audience in  the context of sustainability. His recent work includes the first prize for the Farini competition (60 ha) in Milan in 2019, the 70 hectares Central Park in Taichung, Taiwan, completed in April 2020, the Agora of the French National Radio in Paris, a 2700 m2 Exhibition architecture for Luma Foundation in Arles, France. He has held professorships at GSD Harvard University, Columbia, Cornell and Princeton Universities. He is currently a tenured Associate Professor at the National Superior School of Architecture in Versailles, France (ENSA - V). His work has been exhibited in 2017 at the Chicago and Seoul Architecture  Biennales.

Composer, researcher and conductor, Valerio Sannicandro studied in Cologne and Paris. Winner of several awards (Claudio Abbado Kompositionspreis Berlin in 2014, Music Viva Munich in 2002 and 2010), he searches for a musical language and sonic gesture that are strongly related to space (Ius Lucis, 2007; A Book of Forms, 2014; Ephemeris/Ekléipsis, 2015). He worked in the most representative research studios (IRCAM, Experimentalstudio Freiburg, ZKM, GRM) and completed a Ph.D. at TU Berlin with a research on room-mode analysis applied to musical composition. Affiliated Fellow of the American Academy in Rome, Valerio Sannicandro was invited to Villa Kujoyama in Kyoto from the French Ministry of Culture. He has composed works for voices, orchestra and music theatre with or without electronics, a tool that he uses in a personal and intriguing way. His compositions are performed in Europe, USA, Japan and edited by Suvini-Zerboni (Milan, Italy).
