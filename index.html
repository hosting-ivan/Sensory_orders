John Alderman

(US) designer, author, musician:
THE DISORIENTING PRESENT

Working as an experience strategist, I plan how sensory inputs and outputs can focus an experience and make something more meaningful, useful  or interesting. To improve on this, but also for my own benefit, I practice awareness of my own senses and how, on their own or mediated with  technologies, these senses shift or expand attention, affecting my state of mind and emotion. The work of planning, and the work of attentiveness  have recently come into tension as the limits and pressures of the built sensory environment – home, an outside world, surrounding activity – push  against psychological needs in a disorienting present. I’m less able to focus, but still trying to take it all in and what I receive is a mix of natural and  artificial, each with their own agendas, benign or menacing. As I am alone, they sound and reverberate more strongly.  

 

On one day in a summer of isolation, within my apartment in San Francisco, instability and crisis (2020) mixes with periods of calm and anxiety, and overlays a  sense of order through rhythm. But this way to understand time and experience is not as easy to follow as a heartbeat or simple human breath.

Michael Bell & Eunjeong Seong

(US & KR) architects & writers :
VISIBLE WEATHER

For much of our life in architecture there has been a foreboding sense that money eclipses and precedes most experience. The sensory qualities   of life that are vivid not only seem to be but are literally unanchored; routinely life splitting if you allow the physiology of sensory experience. Those   that theorize the nervous system – such as art historian Jonathan Crary – convey mediated stimuli virtually autonomous of the socially fabricated   meanings that are made concurrent to disguise the damage. A sensory person dismantled by stimuli – not fulfilled. All of this begins to fall apart,   however, if you imagine a world a mere 20 years from now: people far from distant urban life, living in shelters that are a downstream by-product of   solar energy. Rather than being subjects of a divided nervous system, urban media and capital, we imagine living aside near-automatic solar energy   generation. A house that does not know you are home. The spectrum of light (and chemistry) at play does not register to the human eye. Leaving   the would-be subject unknown in the solar nexus. Alone for the first time in two centuries: adjacent to the instrumental rather than within it. After architecture. After real estate. After scarcity.  

 

Bell-Seong/Visible Weather, Encrypting The Sun + Housing After Banking (2020)

Jennifer Biddle

(US/AU) anthropologist:
LESS ‑ BLACK SUMMER SYDNEY 2019-2020

Aboriginal peoples and country were some of the most devastated by the 2019-2020 bushfires of Australia; their presence and perspectives marginalised   and subsumed within national reporting†. Less, my work of creative non-fiction here attests to this history. An experiment in ethnography, it evokes an   encounter with white female sensory annihilation to evidence the endurance of colonial inequities and radically disparate terms of lived experience, perspective and privilege in the making of the event. 

 

B. Williamson et al, Aboriginal peoples and the response to the 2019–2020 Bushfires CAEPR Working Paper 134/2020; Allam, L. For First Nations people the bushfires bring a particular grief, burning what makes us who we are. 'The Guardian' (January 5, 2020).

Oron Catts & Ionat Zurr

(IL/AU) artists & researchers working in the biological arts :
THERMOCEPTION

In these strange times, sensing heat is used to control the movement of people. Mammals (and birds) evolved to maintain and regulate their body’s temperature in near optimal condition for biological processes. When something goes wrong, such as in the case of COVID-19, the body often responds   by raising its temperature. This change in temperature is now used as part of the biopolitical theatre of control and containment, executed through   our constructed technological heat measurement instrument – the thermometer. But the technology used to maintain and regulate temperature is the   thermostat, invented in 1620 by Cornelis Drebbel to control the temperatures of the chicken incubator. The thermostat is considered to be the first (first order) cybernetic device, as it allows for feedback control. It is then not surprising that it was used to outsource the care for lifeforms to a “machine.” Our bodies are thus both sensor and regulator: thermometer (through thermoception) and thermostat.

 

Thermoception – studies into the effects of heat sensing on society and biology (2020) photo Oron Catts

Ben Cerveny

(US/NL) urban strategist:
EXCEEDING BOUNDARIES

At this precarious point in history, we may be witnessing the twilight of individual human agency. As a culture, we have thrown ourselves into processes   that generate complex fields of influence and control, emanating from computational, procedural, and organizational forms that exceed the boundaries   of coherent human perception and cognition. These algorithmically driven creations have long since acquired metabolic patterns all their own and now orchestrate humans at scales both inconceivably vast and imperceptibly subtle toward goals almost unknowable in their systemic abstraction. In order to be able to remain a participant in our own unfolding future, humans as a species must strive to build tools that give us lenses onto facets of these systems in order to truly see dependencies and potentialities. Data visualization, simulation in game engines, public software stacks providing privacy and participatory models for collaborative governance; these are the core infrastructures of a new approach to socially-centered computation.

Yangyifan Dong

(CN) graphic designer :
MAKING SENSE OF MYSELF

Being away from my culture to get trained as a designer in another language offers me a new life and practice to build something both similar and   strange. I started over as someone with an empty history who knows little about social norms. I am not more confident, not more ignorant, not freer, or   braver. Life seems to hover between wet and dry, and I decide to focus on making sense of myself and the surroundings, in the hope of finding myself   as a standard for reference. For my 4-year old past, everything I own, I remember how and why I attained them and the price I paid. I numbered all items   with orders based on the most used to the least used as well as the time these occupied and the pleasure they gave. An abstract subject such as my   life gets to be solidified by keeping a personal recording – an act of organizing the unorganized.  

 

Self-mapping (2019) project in mixed media

Christoph Engemann

(DE) sociologist, digital culture scholar:
SENSING THE SOCIAL

Is there a sense of the social? An awareness of the presence or absence of one’s friends, acquaintances, family, of the wider circle of people one is   affiliated with personally or professionally, of maybe the society one is part of? With the advent of social media messages, postings, log-ins and last   online notifications have become the means by which we sense the social. An outstanding presence on a messenger, a lack of posting on a social   media platform or the simple absence of likes on postings. The machines that enable such social senses constantly sense and order our social   interactions themselves. Each individual is a node on a graph, each interaction an edge between nodes. Centrality measures immediately calculate   the relative distance and importance of any given node, and enable strategies to suggest contacts, contexts and ads. The order of our sense of the social   is thus generated at the intersection of economic, political and machinic dynamics.  

 

Experimental setup developed at the MIT Group Research Laboratory during the late 1940’s which gave rise to the development of centrality measures. The setup  consists of a round table sitting five participants separated by screens. Tasked to order a set of cards they were only allowed to communicate via leaflets  passed through slits in the screens. Once order had been established the participants could click a switch connected to a light panel in a different room. The epistemology of the social of this setup eventually lead to social media like Facebook, Twitter and Instagram & TikTok.

John Alderman is a design and brand strategist, writer, and musician. He has covered the senses in creativity, design, business, and technology in books, magazines, and his daily work and consulting practice. His book Designing Across Senses: A Multimodal Approach to Product Design offers designers an introduction and toolset to approach designing for new contexts. As a musician he has performed worldwide with Three Day Stubble, a legendary nerd rock band, and continues with many collaborations, solo works, and as Mr. Hungry and the Gnawing Sensations. 

Bell Seong Architecture was established in 2010 in New York. We are based in New York City with a satellite studio in Berkeley, California. In joining with Eunjeong Seong Michael Bell re-launched the practice he founded in 1988 in Berkeley. Visible Weather was founded in 2011 by Bell and Seong  as a platform form collaborative design in energy, engineering, materials science, economics and advanced manufacturing. Our partners have included Arup, Transsolar, Lafarge, Oldcastle Glass and manufacturing/tech companies in the Bay Area. Bell is a professor at Columbia University, a visiting professor at Stanford, and has taught at Berkeley and Harvard. Seong teaches at Pratt Institute.
