Nitin Shroff

(IN/SEY/UK/FR) post-colonial artist:
BOUNDARIES EVERYWHERE…

Rough: The other day when I was returning from the sea, I arrived at a village with swastikas on road signs. We are in a region with a millennium long tradition of migration.Today, the Mediterranean is the largest cemetery in Europe.  There is a retinal impact when you see such signs. They construct partitions with the “others.” They change the way you see everything. Even when the sea is no longer the boundary, you see boundaries everywhere. This is the kind of sensory politics that I work with. I think of planetary relationships as well as mothers and sons. The sensory shocks of everyday life and the liminal sensing we never notice.

 

Mother and Son (2002) Infrared is used to capture the universal nature of the bond between the two. Being silenced, the dialogue is left open and ambiguous. Mother and Son references technology and popular culture, surveillance and war.

TeZ

(IT/NL) artist, composer, independent researcher:
THE LABORATORY AS SENSING BODY

From the “cave” of the Laboratory, the imagination of the artist probes the fringes of the Universe to explore the mystery of life. I conceive the studio as a laboratory of co-creation – between machines and living organisms. The Laboratory is a morphing experiment where the sensing body of the artist is informed at liminal and subliminal levels and where tools and instruments are the laboratory’s skeletal and nervous systems. These apparatuses are means of “construction” at physical, intellectual and metaphysical levels. Thus, the relationship between machine and human is not simply   instrumental. It is sensorial and affective. The laboratory’s machines embed features of my own sensory abilities which I then can use to extend my   perception into invisible domains – organic and inorganic manifestations of life which operate beyond my sensory envelopes. This interplay between human perception and machinic sensing translates into new forms of engagement with the outside world.

 

Aquatic State (2019) water, custom cryogenic microscopy device, OpenCV software (video and sound developed in collaboration with Federico Murgia) 

John Thackara

(UK/FR) journalist, writer, curator:
EXPERIENCING RELATIONS AND CONNECTIONS

My theory of change is borrowed from Ilya Prigogine: “When a system is far from equilibrium, small islands of coherence have the capacity to shift the   entire system”. As a writer, my work therefore involves a search for small islands of coherence – that I can later describe – in which social and ecological relationships thrive together. My aim as a curator is similar : I strive to enable embodied encounters with situations (or ‘islands’) in which we feel ourselves   to be part of nature, rather than separate from it. This work is therefore not symbolic, like ‘systems thinking’. It’s more field work, than head work. I want people to experience relational ecologies, not just think about them. I have a hunch that tools like phenocams, when used on-site, can be used to enhance embodied experience. The artist Eva Bakkeslett describes this process – the cultivation of ecological and social connectivity – as social fermentation.

 

Ania Zoltkowski, Fashion Ecologies (2018)

Jesus Torres

(SP/FR) architect, musician:
SOUND AS URBAN SPACE IMAGE

Architecture is vibration as sound is an urban space image. In acoustics, the sonorous behavior is analyzed from different elements, the sources and   the acoustic conditions of the material constructing the “box.” This kind of analysis considers the nature of the shape that receives the source and the   absorption coefficient of each material involved. The urban space is represented by dominant frequencies that are related to the specific geometry of the space. They reproduce a superposition as serial   symphonic sounds. A note is the construction of a series of 16 notes. The dominant frequencies are constructing our experience of the city beyond what we perceive. Each space, architectural, or urban, has their own dominant frequencies like a musical instrument. In that sense, the architect may   be understood as a luthier. Hearing the city is being in an instrument articulated by its infrastructures, densities and spacialities. From neighborhoods to hallways, the vibrations   express a continuous movement and hierarchy of fluxes, a superposition of different spaces each with different sounds and “times.”

 

 Fréquences Temporelles: Partitions – Hong Kong, Paris and San Francisco 

Ignacio Valero

(CL/US) philosopher, economist:
PHARMAKON OF ENTANGLEMENTS

Lacan’s Real, that which resists representation, “is impossible,” yet suddenly confronting its materiality is traumatic, especially as the emotional   exploitation and quantified sensory dislocation of the new EMOTARIAT under a digital hyperreal. Enter coronavirus-SARS-CoV-2, geopolitics, massive inequality under a metastasizing neoliberal regime, authoritarian populisms, and climate change, and we have the ingredients for abject trauma on a   global scale. This combination of foreboding ramifications has turned dystopian and apocalyptic, both as “extinction/revelation.” The Judeo-Christian Paradise forces a choice: the “Tree of Life” or the “Tree of Knowledge of Good and Evil. Plato’s ‘shadows’ or Eikasia (eikon-icon-image) in the Allegory of the Cave are no less dualistic than Maya (‘illusion’) in Vedanta Hinduism, or the ‘ten thousand things’ in Taoism. But a baby’s recursive primal scream   reenacts that conundrum: the collapse of the wave function’s undecidable quantum entanglement, poison and remedy. EcoDomics, as an “art of living and making (in) common(s), might help harmonize the sensory loss we are now experiencing.

 

Eco-Domics, the Emotariat, and the Aesthetic(s) of the Common(s) 

Nina Wakeford

(UK) sociologist, artist:
WE WILL REPLACE ALL MEN WITH MACHINES

Now we must activate the unfinished business of feminists who addressed scientific and technological change in the 1970s and 1980s. We must   develop our sensory order by re-engaging with all that undetonated energy, as we search for each other through our screens. We have noticed that there is a tendency to behave as if we are ‘beyond’ previous demands to overthrow patriarchy. Even in our state of crisis ordinariness, both science and popular culture are dominated by the generation of anticipation, speculation and optimization of bodies. We return to Cynthia Cockburn’s book Brothers: Male Dominance and Technological Change which demonstrated how male workers maintained power over women as manual tasks were replaced by machines. We can compare this to Shulamith Firestone’s optimism in The Dialectic of Sex: The Case for Feminist Revolution that new technology would free women’s bodies from heteropatriarchal control. We will listen again to Jayaben Desai’s declaration, as she led the industrial dispute of women at the   photo processing facilities at Grunwick in 1976: “We are the lions, Mr Manager.”  

 

Still from performance We Will Replace all Men with Machines, Barbican Centre, London (2019)

Nitin Shroff is an Indian-Seychellois artist born in Mumbai, India. Working in a variety of disciplines including time-based and photography and Found-Painting and writing. In 2015 he proposed and collaborated on the first National Pavilion of the Seychelles. La Biennale di Venezia. 56th International Art Exhibition. He is currently based in rural southern France, working on several international personal and collaborative projects concerning memory and language.

TeZ is Italian interdisciplinary artist, musician and independent researcher, has been living in the Netherlands since 2002. His work, in the form of installations and performances, probes the intersections between  Art, Design, Science and Technology with a particular focus on immersive environments, cross-modal  perception and sensory plasticity. TeZ is also the brainfather of Optofonica Lab for Synesthetic  ArtScience, based in Amsterdam and currently involved in projects investigating new forms of  relationships between living organisms and artificial agents.

John Thackara is a philosopher, writer and curator, working in the realms of social, ecological and relational design. He curated the celebrated Doors of Perception conference for 20 years, first in Amsterdam, later across India; he was commissioner of the UK social innovation biennial Dott 07, and the French design biennial City Eco Lab; and in 2019 curated the Urban-Rural expo in Shanghai. He is a Senior Fellow at the Royal College of Art, Visiting Professor at Tongji University in Shanghai, and curator of the Social Food Forum. His last book - How To Thrive In the Next Economy: Designing Tomorrow’s World Today - has just been published in China.

Jesús Torres García was born in Granada, Spain, in 1977. While only eight years old, he attended the National Conservatory of Music and later played and  recorded with leading Spanish artists.  His architecture relates to music and  writing while using body and landscape references. He works with the narrative  techniques of architecture to explore notions of anthropological, historical and  sensorial space. Shapes are manipulated into fields of symbols and harmonies to achieve scenographic and sculptural, expressions.He has taught at the ETSA  Granada, ENSA Paris-Malaquais and ENSA Paris-Belleville, and was a visiting  Scholar at the Faculty of Architecture in Hong Kong.

Ignacio Valero PhD, UC Berkeley; Diploma ITC-UNESCO Centre; MAT (Sociology) University of Florida; B.S. (Economics) Missouri State University. Currently: Associate Professor, California College of the Arts/San Francisco. Formerly: Dean, School of Fine Arts, CCAC; member of presidential advisory council on Sustainable Development for new Colombian Constitution; Acting Director/Deputy Director, Colombia’s Environmental Agency-INDERENA; Latin American Environment/Education Coordinator, CIFCA/UNEP, Madrid; Sierra Nevada de Santa Marta Eco-Development Pilot Project Coordinator; Environment/Science/Technology Research Fellow, COLCIENCIAS; Environmental Consultant IDB/US–AID/UNDP. Research: The Emotariat, EcoDomics and the Aesthetic(s) of the Common(s); Biopolitics, Political Ecology, Governance; Neoliberal globalization, Emotional Platform Capitalism, Affective Labor, Social Media, Populism, Post-Truth and the Society of the Spectacle; Science Fiction and Techno-Fetishism; Social Movements, Other Worlds and the Global South. 

Nina Wakeford is Reader in Fine Art at Goldsmiths, University of London where she also convenes the MA Visual Sociology. She makes work that begins with the unfinished business of past social movements, and the challenges of revisiting the energies that these movements created, and has also made contributions to Science and Technology Studies. She is interested in how identification and disidentification occur, empathy and inhabitation, and the risks of staying loyal or respectful to the kinds of materials that initiate her work. Wakeford is co-editor of Inventive Methods: The Happening of the Social (Routledge, 2012), a collection which explores how research might better work with openness and ambiguity.
