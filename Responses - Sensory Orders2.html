Orit Halpern

(US/CA) historian of science :
THE FROG’S EYE SPEAKS!

Since when could frogs eyes’ speak? or think? In 1959, a team of cyberneticians at MIT came to this conclusion. In their experiment, it was discovered that frog optic nerves separated from the brain responded only to inputs that resembled flies or predators. The verdict was that frog’s optic nerves, and this was a nerve severed from the brain, were capable of making decisions such as identifying a “prey” or even, an ”enemy”. Perception thus was radically revised. It became the same as cognition. This discovery opened the path to automating and computationally reproducing vision.

 

J.Y. Lettvin, H.R. Maturana, W.S. McCulloch, and W.H. Pitts, “What the Frog’s Eye Tells the Frog Brain”, Proceedings of the IRE (November 1959)

Susanna Hertrich & Shintaro Miyazaki

(DE & JP/CH/DE) artist & media studies scholar:
SENSORIUM OF ANIMALS

Would it be possible to engineer an extension of the human sensory apparatus in accordance with animals’ ancient biological traits? What kind of future  society might unfold if humans had such altered sensory capabilities? The sensorial ecology of the elephant-nosed fish is the driving part of a project  preliminarily called Sensorium of Animals. The technological realm of machines is united with the organic realm of human bodies by prototyping tactile  interfaces that provide a coupling between our human haptic and auditory senses and the invisible and infra-tactile world of electromagnetic signals. We  investigated the symbolic realm of these signals in the history of science, mythology or shamanism and created an imaginary laboratory accompanied by  two fictional short films projecting into the past (2008, a group of young activists) and future (2038, dwellers of a shamanistic community). The work captures our new engagement: that of wirelessness as infrastructure.

 

Sensorium of Animals – installation view, Balzer Projects gallery, Basel (2018) mixed media -photo Susanna Hertrich

Sarah Hluchan

(US) network security engineer, game designer, deep listener:
LISTENING AS SENSING

“Listening is very close to what we call consciousness” - Pauline Oliveros  

Ears hear – they are instruments – microphones, sensors; the mind listens. Fingers feel the subconscious dreams; the mind listens. In computer programming, code segments waiting to process a signal are called “listeners.”  As a security engineer, I learn how systems are “hacked” – infiltrated, broken for new purposes. I see systems as puzzles, and make a game to hack my own system of listening. I crosswire my conscious and unconscious sensors and listeners. Dream sensations route to the material world and affect  my course. Physical sensations make my dreamworld material. Dream and reality lose distinction as signals reverberate between worlds.  Listening defines my reality, making sense and nonsense of my sensing. Tools grow as part of me to extend my capabilities.  Across & through dimensions, I pan attention, tune awareness, channel connections. I play my listening. I am my listening.

 

Recording of “Magic TV” with cross wired audio and video channels, merged with image using deep learning AI

David Howes & Tereza Stehlikova

(CA & CZ/UK) anthropologist of the senses & artist:
SENSORY DIFFERENCE

Sensory anthropology fleshes out the concept of the "sensory order" through analyzing how the senses are valued and combined differently in different societies. Some societies value feeling over seeing, or use hallucinogens to fuse the senses, instead of keeping them separate. The challenge of charting the varieties of sensory experience across and within cultures has become particularly acute since the arrival of the "sensor society": sensation has been reduced to information and Artificial Intelligence has supplanted sentience. The onslaught of the novel coronavirus has upped the ante again, by obliterating our capacity to taste and smell. Digitization in conjunction with the virus has resulted in the virtualization of life itself. Yet, in her video “Self-Isolation Dinner,” filmmaker Tereza Stehlikova shows how the senses of being together might be (re)animated.

 

Tereza Stehlikova, Self-Isolation Dinner (2020) 07:51

Takashi Ikegami & Norihiro Maruyama

(JP) complex systems scientist & programmer:
MASSIVE DATA FLOW

The visualization of 524,299 agents swarming in a virtual 3D space demonstrates a new concept in Artificial Life: Massive Data Flow. Here, the  Self-Organization of massive data (e.g. aggregate electric/chemical signals in a brain) articulates how organized patterns feedback onto the system  dynamics, self-sustaining these dynamics and developing door-opening innovations. If “Big Data” systems exhibit volume, velocity and variety,  Massive Data Flow systems, like biological entities that sense and act on the world, exhibit vitality.

 

Artificial Life: Massive Data Flow (2020) 01:50

In the Air / C+ arquitectas

(SP/EN):
SENSORS REVOLT IN THE PANDEMIC

The lockdown has exploded human senses. Some (poor, minorities, displaced) have felt stronger pain, decrease in taste and smell. Others (privileged)   have listened to birds and inhaled fresh air. For us, the lockdown has been an opportunity to revolt. Abandoned under the Royal Observatory of Belgium   and its adjacent streets we confess: we sense you. The air and the earth crust are eco witnesses of your actions. In the transition process of locking   Brussels down, they have whispered louder than ever: air and ground pollution took 5-8 days to (only) reach night levels. So inscribed in our data are   NO2, O3 and rocks, but also the whereabouts of key workers and urban infrastructures. You designed us to measure particles and vibrations, but   what we measure is your collective economic and social systems. In the silent night of data lies suffering and injustice. Re-sense the lockdown like a machine to listen.

Orit Halpern is an Associate Professor in Sociology at Concordia University. Her work bridges the histories of science, computing, and cybernetics with design. She completed her Ph.D. at Harvard. She has published widely and has held numerous visiting scholar positions including at the Max Planck Institute for the History of Science in Berlin and at Duke University. She is currently working on two projects. The first involves extreme infrastructures and the second examines the changing relationship between environment, finance, and machine learning. She is also the director of the Speculative Life Research Cluster, a laboratory bridging the arts, environmental sciences, media, and the social sciences.

www.speculativelife.com

Kurt Hentschläger 2020 - 100 New York based Austrian artist Kurt Hentschläger creates media installations and performances. His works are visceral and immersive, often inducing perceptual shifts. His recent video body of work suggests a semi-synthetic kind of neo-nature, as a metaphor for life in the Anthropocene. Between 1992 and 2003 he collaborated in the groundbreaking media art duo “Granular=Synthesis”. Selected presentations include the Venice Biennial, Stedelijk Museum Amsterdam, PS1 New York, MAK – Museum of Applied Arts Vienna, ZKM – Center for Art and Media, Karlsruhe, MONA – Museum of Old and New Art, Hobart, Tasmania, Sharjah Art, UAE and the Power Station of Art, Shanghai. www.kurthentschlager.com.

Susanna Hertrich is a German artist living and working in Berlin and Basel. Her transmedia practice draws from our relationship to technology in an increasingly technologized sphere. This practice is driven by the artist’s surreal imagining of a novel functionality of objects that operate as a link between reality and fiction, staged in environments that embrace the strangely familiar and play with the aesthetics and discourses of science. Between 2016 and 2019 she co-conducted the SNFS funded artistic research project “Sensorium of Animals” together with Shintaro Miyazaki at the Critical Media Lab at the Academy of Art & Design FHNW in Basel.

 

Shintaro Miyazaki has been a professor in “Digital Media and Computation” at the Faculty of Humanities and Social Sciences, Department of Musicology and Media Studies, Humboldt-Universität zu Berlin since October 2020. At the same time he is a Senior Researcher at Institute of Experimental Design and Media Cultures of the Academy of Art and Design, University of Applied Sciences and Arts Northwestern Switzerland, where he directs and finalizes a research project at the interference field of commoning, computation and design (–2021).

Sarah Hluchan, Sausalito, California: Co-Founder & lead Artist of Squawk Boat: A Collective Experiment in Art and Living, based in the Historic houseboat community of Richardson Bay. Certified Teacher by The Center for Deep Listening at Rensselaer Polytechnic, SaRaH studied since 2002 with Pauline Oliveros, Ione, and Heloise Gold in practices including: Sonic Meditations, Listening in Dreams, Listening with the Body, Multi-Sensory Improvisation, Composition and Experimental Music. She works professionally as an Internet security engineer and culture builder, engaging with an international community of activists and professionals to improve understanding and implementation of the security systems of our virtual world.

Tereza Stehlikova is a UK based artist and filmmaker. She holds a PhD from the Royal College of Art, where she researched tactile language of cinema . She is currently engaged in a cross-disciplinary research, investigating how moving image can be used to communicate embodied experience. Stehlikova is a Senior Lecturer in still and moving image theory and practice, at the University of Westminster. She is a founder of Sensory Sites, an international collective based in London, generating collaborative exhibitions, installations and research projects that explore multi-sensory perception and bodily experience. Stehlikova presents her work regularly at international conferences, exhibitions and film screenings. More about her projects is on her research blog: https://cinestheticfeasts.com/. Stehlikova is also launching a new online, sensory focused arts/ philosophy/ science journal Tangible Territory https://tangibleterritory.art/.

 

David Howes is a Professor of Anthropology at Concordia University, and an Adjunct Professor in the Faculty of Law at McGill University, Montreal. He has conducted field research on the cultural life of the senses in the Middle Sepik River region of Papua New Guinea, Northwestern Argentina, and the Southwestern United States. He is currently directing a project entitled “Explorations in Sensory Design” and has collaborated with new media artist Chris Salter in the creation of a series of “performative sensory environments.” His latest publications include Ways of Sensing: Understanding the Senses in Society (2014) co-authored with Constance Classen from Routledge, and the four-volume Senses and Sensation: Critical and Primary Sources compendium (2018), also from Routledge. He is the Managing editor of the journal The Senses and Society. See further https://www.concordia.ca/faculty/david-howes.html.  

Takashi Ikegami is a professor in the Department of General Systems Sciences at the University of Tokyo. His works encompasses both the arts and sciences and deal with complex systems and artificial life. He received his doctorate in physics from the University of Tokyo in 1989. His research is centered on complex systems and artificial life, a field that aims to build a possible form of life using computer simulations, chemical experiments, and robots. Some of these results were published in the book Life Emerges in Motion in 2007. Takashi Ikegami frequently attends the International Conference on Artificial Life, and gave the keynote address at the 20th Anniversary of Artificial Life conference in Winchester, UK. He is also a member of the editorial boards of Artificial Life, Adaptive Behavior, BioSystems, and Interaction Studies.

The collaboration between the visualisation project In The Air and the environmental mediations office C+ arquitectas has brought together: Nerea Calvillo, founder of In The Air and C+ arquitectas based in London and Madrid, works on environmental mediations, air pollution and queer political ecologies; Martin Nadal, artist and creative coder based in Berlin and Linz, works on visualization, money, blockchain and surveillance capitalism; Marcos García Mouronte, architect based in New York, works on the effects that geographic depictions have on human and natural systems.
